Glaucoma Treatments and Therapies

It has no cure, but glaucoma can be successfully managed, or treated, if found early. Left untreated, glaucoma can lead to vision loss and blindness.

Medical treatments for glaucoma can help slow down the progress of the disease. If you get a glaucoma diagnosis and your vision hasn’t been affected, a successful treatment plan may help minimize symptoms for many years to come.

Early diagnosis means your doctor can start treatments right away. Unfortunately, if you’ve already lost some of your vision, no amount of treatment can bring it back. This is why annual exams are so important.

  • Treatments for glaucoma may include medicated eye drops, oral medications, or surgery to help reduce the pressure at the front of your eye.
  • Your eye doctor may prescribe eye drops and oral medications first.
  • Surgery isn’t usually recommended until all other options have been exhausted.

Before your glaucoma treatment begins, make sure your doctor knows your complete health history and any medications you’re taking.

Medicated Eye Drops and Oral Medications

Most doctors prescribe daily eye drops first, and it has a high success rate. If medicated eye drops don’t decrease eye pressure, your doctor may recommend oral medications. A combination of treatments may be used, as well, such as daily eye drops, along with prescription medication, which is often enough to relieve the pressure. Regular check-ups to monitor the pressure are often all that is needed to keep your eyes healthy over time.

Types of Medications

The following list of glaucoma medications is provided by the Glaucoma Research Foundation.

They’re used to slow down the flow of the fluid toward the front of the eye or provide a way for the fluid to drain away from it. It should be noted that using eye drops or medications can have some side effects, but most people can use them without any issues.

  • Prostaglandin analogs: Travatan® Z, Lumigan®, Zioptan™, Xalatan®
  • Beta blockers: Timolol Maleate USP, Betoptic® S, Betagan®, OptiPranolol®, Istalol®, Timoptic-XE®, Betimol®
  • Alpha agonists: Iopidine®, Alphagan®
  • Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (CAIs): Azopt®, Neptazane®, Trusopt®, Diamox® Sequels®
  • Cholinergics: Isopto® Carpine, Isopto® Carbachol, Pilopine HS® Gel, Pilocarpine HCl Ophthalmic Solution USP

Laser Procedures or Surgery

Sometimes, if medications don’t work, your doctor may recommend laser surgery. Depending on the procedure, you may be able to get this done at your doctor’s office. If not, you can go to a medical center or hospital and have the laser procedure on an outpatient basis. There is no incision made during laser surgery. It’s all done with a beam of light that alters the tissue in the eye to relieve pressure.

Having one laser surgery may keep your eye pressure at an acceptable level. However, your eye doctor may ask you to continue the medicated eye drops and oral medications even after the surgery; a combination of treatments is often necessary to control the disease. You may even need another laser surgery down the road.

Operating Room Surgery

Incisional surgery is usually the last treatment offered if medicated eye drops, oral medications, or laser surgery haven’t controlled the pressure against the front of the eye. Typically, two types of surgery are offered: a trabeculectomy or the addition of draining devices.

Trabeculectomy

During a trabeculectomy, the surgeon creates a tiny flap on the white of your eye. This flap forms a pocket where the fluid can escape to help lower eye pressure.

Draining Devices

If your eye surgeon chooses a draining device instead of a trabeculectomy, a tiny drainage tube is implanted in your eye. This tube redirects the fluid so it can be absorbed into nearby blood vessels.

Glaucoma New Treatments & Therapies

New treatments are being developed that could change the future of glaucoma care. These technologies may improve how current treatments are administered. Or, they may provide a new way to treat the disease.

Micropumps and Medicated Contact Lenses

Two problems glaucoma patients often have is remembering to put their drops in every day, or putting them in properly so all of their medication is absorbed. Thankfully, new technologies are being tested that may eliminate both of these issues.

Micropump

This device is implanted directly into the eye and medication is controlled by a wireless device.

Medicated contact lenses

These contacts have a layer of medication on the inside of the lens that is released slowly throughout the day.

Micro-stent surgery

Operating room surgery where a device is implanted into the eye to redirect the flow of fluid away from the front of the eye have been used for about a decade, but new implants using stents are now being manufactured. Some of the goals of this treatment include a shorter surgery, less destruction of eye tissue, and a faster postoperative recovery.  

Clinical Trials

According to ClinicalTrials.gov, a service of the National Institutes of Health, there are currently (as of August 14, 2017) 167 clinical trials for glaucoma being conducted around the globe. These trials are in various stages of development from recruiting to not yet recruiting.

Listed below are a few of the clinical trials that are currently recruiting across the globe:

 

  • Functional Brain Changes of Primary Open-angle Glaucoma Patients
  • Safety & Effectiveness Study of the Hydrus Device for Lowering IOP in Glaucoma Patients Undergoing Cataract Surgery
  • Prevalence of Obstructive Sleep Apnoea in Glaucoma (POSAG)

If you’re interested in a clinical trial, consult with your ophthalmologist to see if this is a feasible option for you.

Alternative Treatments

There are no studies that alternative treatments work, but according to the Glaucoma Research Foundation, “50 percent of glaucoma patients use some form of alternative therapy.”

The Glaucoma Research Foundation lists the following as the most common alternative treatments14:

  • Vitamins A, B1, C, and E: These vitamins show little to no effect on decreasing the pressure in the eye.
  • Marijuana: Long-term studies on this controversial treatment show that it has no proven effect. While it does decrease pressure to the eye, it only does so for a short amount of time.
  • Herbal Remedies: Ginkgo biloba has been shown to increase blood flow in the eye, which may help protect nerve cells against damage.
  • Diet: Eating healthy is always a good idea, but a person’s weight does not impact pressure in the eye.
  • Meditation: Over time, a regular meditation routine may help reduce pressure, but it’s not significant.
  • Acupuncture: There have been many acupuncture studies conducted, but only one showed a minor change in vision and none showed any effect on eye pressure.
  • Exercise: A regular, moderate exercise routine has been shown to reduce eye pressure that may last a few months.   

Speak to your eye doctor before you try any alternative treatments.   

Early Detection is the Best “Treatment”

While not exactly a treatment, seeing your eye doctor on a routine basis is the best defense against advanced glaucoma. Keep your eyes healthy—schedule your routine exam today.
