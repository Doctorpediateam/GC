Glaucoma - Nutrition & Fitness

Nutrition

Researchers are still studying the connection between diet and glaucoma. Here's what we know so far: the way to treat glaucoma is to reduce the eye pressure.

We already know that excess salt can raise your blood pressure. High blood pressure can increase the pressure in the eye, which makes glaucoma worse. So if you either have glaucoma or are at risk for glaucoma, using a moderate amount of salt in your food has been and continues to be a standard dietary recommendation.

Coffee and caffeinated drinks are also known to cause at least a temporary spike in eye pressure. Ask your doctor if that puts you at risk and if you should switch to decaf.

On the flip side, alcohol can reduce eye pressure temporarily. But there is no evidence that drinking alcohol reduces your glaucoma risk.

As for what helps, some large population studies found that a diet rich in retinol, a form of vitamin A, helps to reduce your risk of glaucoma. Foods with retinol include milk, liver, cheese, and butter. Even though those foods may contain fat, there was no evidence that a diet rich in dietary fats played any role in promoting glaucoma. But an excessive intake of fats is known to contribute to obesity and cardiovascular disease.

The researchers also saw a correlation between lower rates of glaucoma and a diet high in leafy green veggies and fruits like peaches and apricots.

African-American women in that study had a decreased risk of glaucoma by eating fruits and veggies rich in vitamin A, vitamin C, and carotenoids.

Doctors also recommend eating fish or nuts rich in omega-3 fatty acids, which appear to reduce risk. You should not drink large amounts of liquids at one time. Drink small amounts over the course of your day. Add moderate amounts of red wine, dark chocolate, and green tea. In fact, some Doctors recommend the same foods that make up a heart-healthy diet to their patients who ask about diet and glaucoma.

Fitness

It turns out that fitness may play a role in glaucoma too. Experts believe aerobic exercise can help lower eye pressure. It also helps reduce other glaucoma risk factors like diabetes and heart disease. Some studies show exercise may even improve blood flow to your retina and optic nerve.

Good news: it doesn't have to be strenuous exercise. It can be as simple as a brisk 20-minute walk four times a week.

One note of caution. Some rare forms of glaucoma are not responsive to exercise. And vigorous exercise may increase the pressure in your eye with some types of glaucoma. It's best to check in with your eye doctor before beginning your exercise program. Remember that exercise is not a substitute for medication and treatment.
